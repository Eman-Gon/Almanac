# Hackathon Context

## Official challenge

Build a solution using **AI agents** to substantially improve or transform the effectiveness of food-bank supply chains. The official scope includes:

- Accepting donations
- Purchasing food
- Warehousing food
- Allocating food
- Packing food
- Distributing food
- Delivering food

Solutions designed for a specific food bank are encouraged.

## Operating philosophy

The challenge materials state that technology should make workers better at their jobs rather than attempt to do their jobs for them. Product behavior should therefore be:

- Assistive
- Explainable
- Reviewable
- Reversible
- Human-approved
- Grounded in actual operational constraints

## Food-bank operating scale described in the challenge

The Alameda County material characterizes food banking as a real supply chain involving approximately:

- 20 inbound trucks per day
- More than 100,000 square feet of warehouse space
- A fleet leaving every morning
- Roughly 60 million pounds of food per year
- Hundreds of partner agencies
- Volatile and seasonal supply meeting persistent demand

These figures are context from the challenge material, not live application data.

## Seven official opportunity themes

### 1. See It Coming

- Forecast supply volume and mix
- Score donors and vendors on actual delivery performance
- Identify advantageous purchase windows
- Predict actual truck arrival times

### 2. The Warehouse That Explains Itself

- Surface receiving data at the dock
- Recommend storage locations
- Sequence picking efficiently
- Flag product condition and shelf-life risk
- Target likely inventory discrepancies
- Predict equipment failures

### 3. Production Is Manufacturing

- Forecast packing demand
- Recommend line setup and crew size
- Predict volunteer attendance
- Stage materials before production
- Track yield and waste

### 4. Equity With a Truck Attached

- Route using receiving windows and product urgency
- Balance driver workload using stop difficulty
- Rebuild the day after vehicle or agency disruption
- Coordinate vehicle-energy constraints
- Learn from refusals and short receipts

### 5. Need Is the Demand Signal

- Detect mismatch between agency ordering and community need
- Use pull-based replenishment signals
- Detect access friction before participation falls
- Match inbound food to preferences and dietary patterns
- Present pantry information in clear language

### 6. Every Team Member an Analyst

- Allow plain-language operational questions
- Brief leads at shift start
- Convert voice notes into structured proposals
- Capture long-tenured staff knowledge

### 7. A Smarter Movement

- Benchmark operations across food banks
- Match regional surplus to shortage
- Model policy and economic changes by neighborhood

## What judges are likely to value

The public deck does not provide a weighted rubric, so the following are informed judging assumptions:

1. A real food-bank problem
2. Clear usefulness to staff
3. Meaningful agent behavior beyond a chatbot
4. A working end-to-end prototype
5. Quantifiable value
6. Strong explanation and presentation
7. Responsible human control

Do not claim these percentages or weights as official.

## Presentation constraints

- At least one team representative must present onsite.
- The demo should be understandable without detailed technical narration.
- Use one coherent operational story.
- Explain what is real, what is simulated, and what would require integration in production.
- Quantify both the problem and the solution.
